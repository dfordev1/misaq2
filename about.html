Method & how to read this

Covenant is treated here as a structural category, not a topic. The claim is that ʿahd and mīthāq organise a great deal of the revelation that is usually read piecemeal — and that seeing the structure changes how the parts read.

Source hierarchy

Claims are grounded in this order: the Qurʾān; then ṣaḥīḥ sunnah; then the classical tafsīr tradition; then kalām, taṣawwuf and fiqh positions, each tagged to its school rather than presented as the position; then modern scholarship. Where the tradition genuinely disagrees, the disagreement is shown as a fork rather than resolved by preference.

    The diagram templates

    Ten recurring shapes, each chosen because the idea is genuinely that shape — a duty running between two parties, a breach set against a fulfilment, a process, a hub of obligations, a weighing, a trust held in a vessel, a foundation stack, a decision gate, a cycle of renewal, a grid of parallel claims.

    Limits

    Every applied diagram is an ijtihādī application, not a fatwā. A citation shows where the principle is grounded; it does not certify that this application is the only defensible reading of it. For anything consequential, ask someone qualified who knows your circumstances.

    The applications skew heavily interpersonal — most of ordinary life is — so the prophetic and scripture-community axes appear far less often here than they do in the underlying corpus.

    Typography

    Urdu is set in Noto Nastaliq Urdu. Qurʾānic Arabic is deliberately set in Naskh, not Nastaliq, following convention. Citations stay in Latin numerals, the form scholars cite by.

    A note on authority. Every diagram and note here is an ijtihādī study aid, not a fatwā. Citations point to where a principle is grounded; they do not certify that the application is the only defensible reading.
